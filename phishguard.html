What Is Phishing?
📖Overview

Phishing is a cyberattack where an adversary impersonates a trusted entity — a bank, tech company, or colleague — to trick you into revealing credentials, installing malware, or transferring money. It's the most common initial access vector in modern cybercrime, responsible for over a third of all data breaches.

Unlike technical exploits, phishing attacks human psychology. Urgency, authority, fear, and trust are the real vulnerabilities being exploited — not software bugs.

Attack Types
🎣Email-Based
  • PhishingMass campaigns impersonating trusted brands. Low effort, high volume.
  • SpearphishingTargeted attacks using personal details. Much higher success rate.
  • WhalingExecutives targeted. Highly researched — often involves wire transfers.
  • CloneDuplicates a legitimate email you received, with links swapped out.
📱Other Vectors
  • SmishingSMS phishing. Fake delivery alerts, bank OTPs, government texts.
  • VishingPhone call phishing. Fake tech support, IRS agents, bank fraud teams.
  • QuishingQR code phishing. Bypasses URL filters in emails and physical spaces.
  • BECBusiness Email Compromise. CEO/vendor impersonation targeting finance.
Red Flags Checklist
🔍How to Spot a Phishing Email
  • ▸Sender domain doesn't match the brand. "PayPal Security" from paypa1-verify.com or paypal-accounts.ru is the biggest giveaway.
  • ▸Reply-To is different from From. Legitimate emails don't redirect your reply to a random Gmail account.
  • ▸Urgency and pressure. "Act within 24 hours or your account is deleted" is a manipulation tactic, not how real companies communicate.
  • ▸Hover before you click. The display text can say anything. The actual href URL is what matters.
  • ▸Requests for sensitive data. No legitimate company asks for your SSN, password, or card number via email.
  • ▸Unexpected attachments. An .exe, .js, or .zip you weren't expecting is almost certainly malicious.
  • ▸Generic greeting. "Dear Customer" instead of your name suggests a mass campaign, not a personal message.
  • ▸SPF/DKIM failures. If an email claims to be from Google but fails DKIM, Google didn't send it. Check the raw headers.
What Attackers Exploit
🧠Psychological Tactics

Authority — impersonating a CEO, bank, or government agency to make demands feel non-negotiable.

Urgency & Scarcity — "Expires in 24 hours" forces snap decisions that bypass critical thinking.

Fear — threats of account suspension or legal action override rational judgment.

Familiarity — referencing recent transactions or events to seem credible and known.

⚙️Technical Tricks

Typosquatting — paypa1.com, g00gle.com, arnazon.com look correct at a glance.

Homograph attacks — Unicode characters identical to ASCII letters but different code points.

Subdomain abuse — paypal.com.attacker.ru looks like PayPal. The real domain is attacker.ru.

Link shorteners — masking malicious URLs to bypass visual inspection and filters.
